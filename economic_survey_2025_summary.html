EMPLOYMENT AND SKILL DEVELOPMENT EXISTENTIAL PRIORITIES

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy centers on leveraging India's demographic dividend by creating quality jobs and fostering inclusive, sustainable economic growth. This involves aligning the workforce with global demands through reskilling, upskilling, and new-skilling initiatives, while also simplifying compliance and promoting labor flexibility to encourage job creation.

Previous Performance Summary

India experienced substantial employment growth post-pandemic. Unemployment rates declined significantly, and labor force participation and worker population ratios increased. However, the agriculture sector remains dominant, with a rise in self-employment and a shift in women's participation towards self-employment and household enterprises. While factory employment showed resilience, wage growth lagged behind corporate profit growth, raising concerns about income inequality.

Future Predictions

India is projected to become the third-largest economy by 2030, requiring the creation of millions of non-farm jobs annually. The digital economy and renewable energy sectors are identified as key drivers of high-quality job creation. Continued growth hinges on addressing skill gaps, improving wage distribution, and fostering women's participation in the workforce.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include manufacturing, digital economy (including gig work), renewable energy, and traditionally female-dominated sectors like handicrafts and khadi. Companies benefiting include those in these sectors, particularly those embracing technology and promoting women's entrepreneurship. Government initiatives like Startup India and PMKVY are key.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Significant policy changes include the four new Labour Codes aimed at simplifying regulations, increasing labor flexibility, and strengthening worker welfare. Initiatives to promote women's entrepreneurship, skill development programs (PMKVY, JSS, PM Vishwakarma), and the eShram portal for unorganized workers are also highlighted. A new package of five schemes focusing on employment and skilling for 4.1 crore youth over five years was announced.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include a young population, increased formalization, government initiatives promoting entrepreneurship and skill development, and the growth of the digital and renewable energy sectors. Challenges include addressing skill gaps, income inequality (particularly the widening gap between corporate profits and wage growth), and systemic barriers hindering women's workforce participation.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's economic trends are viewed within a global context, comparing its labor market indicators and policies with those of other countries. The document highlights the need for a globally competitive workforce and explores international collaborations to facilitate skilled labor mobility.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Lucrative opportunities exist in the digital economy, renewable energy, and sectors promoting women's entrepreneurship. Investments in skill development initiatives and businesses catering to the growing demand for skilled labor are highlighted.

Technology and Innovation Impact

AI, digital transformation, and emerging technologies are shaping economic policies. The document emphasizes the need for reskilling and upskilling initiatives to prepare the workforce for the changing job landscape, while also acknowledging the potential displacement effects of automation.

Social Implications

Policies aim to improve employment, education, and social welfare, particularly for women and unorganized workers. The focus on skill development, social security schemes (PMSBY, PMJJBY), and initiatives to increase women's workforce participation are key social implications.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's employment growth shows promise, but sustained success requires addressing skill gaps, promoting gender equality, and ensuring fair income distribution. Further reforms are needed to streamline regulations, enhance worker safety, and foster collaboration between industry, academia, and government to fully realize the potential of the demographic dividend. The PM Internship Scheme is a significant step towards bridging the gap between education and employment.

MEDIUM TERM OUTLOOK DEREGULATION DRIVES GROWTH

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy is to achieve 'Viksit Bharat' (developed India) by 2047 through sustained 8% annual GDP growth. This requires a significant increase in investment and focuses on unleashing domestic growth levers, primarily by enhancing economic freedom for individuals and businesses through deregulation.

Previous Performance Summary

India has implemented significant structural reforms in the last decade, including GST, IBC, and the India Stack. While these reforms yielded positive externalities, challenges remain, particularly concerning regulatory burdens hindering MSME growth and employment.

Future Predictions

The IMF projects India to become a $5 trillion economy by FY28 and $6.3 trillion by FY30, with nominal GDP growth around 10.2% in USD terms. However, the report acknowledges the uncertain global environment marked by geo-economic fragmentation and China's growing dominance in manufacturing and energy transition.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include manufacturing (especially EVs and renewable energy), MSMEs, and technology (AI, robotics, biotechnology). Companies focusing on domestic supply chains, particularly in renewable energy and EV components, are likely to benefit. The Mittelstand (SME sector) is identified as crucial for sustainable growth.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes involve systematic deregulation to reduce compliance burdens for MSMEs, simplifying taxation and labor regulations, decriminalizing business laws (Jan Vishwas Act), and promoting digitalization. States are encouraged to adopt a three-step process for deregulation: identifying areas, comparing regulations, and estimating costs.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include domestic investment, increased efficiency, and deregulation. Challenges include geo-economic fragmentation, China's dominance in global supply chains, and the need to balance environmental goals with economic growth. Over-regulation, particularly impacting MSMEs, is a significant hurdle.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's economic trends are analyzed within the context of global geo-economic fragmentation and China's rising economic power. The report compares India's regulatory environment with other countries, highlighting opportunities for learning and reform. The report emphasizes the need for a more inward-looking approach to growth, given global uncertainties.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in domestic supply chains for renewable energy, EVs, and other sectors. Deregulation will create a more favorable investment climate, particularly for MSMEs. The report highlights the potential for growth in India's Mittelstand, mirroring the success of similar sectors in other countries.

Technology and Innovation Impact

Digital transformation (India Stack) is highlighted as a success. The report emphasizes the need for investment in emerging technologies like AI, robotics, and biotechnology to drive future growth. However, the focus is primarily on creating a regulatory environment conducive to innovation, rather than specific technological advancements.

Social Implications

Deregulation aims to boost employment, particularly in the MSME sector. The report acknowledges the need to address the education-employment gap and improve the quality of education. However, a detailed analysis of the social impact of deregulation is not provided.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

The report concludes that systematic deregulation is crucial for achieving India's growth targets. It recommends a three-step process for states to undertake deregulation, focusing on reducing compliance costs, improving enforcement transparency, and adopting risk-based regulation. The report emphasizes the importance of learning from other states and countries' experiences and adopting a 'minimum necessary, maximum feasible' approach to regulation.

EXTERNAL SECTOR GETTING FDI RIGHTL

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy emphasizes resilience and proactive adaptation to global uncertainties. The strategic intent is to enhance India's competitiveness in global trade, attract FDI, and foster sustainable economic growth through deregulation and ease of doing business initiatives. A calibrated approach to industrial policy, using tariffs strategically alongside other tools, is advocated.

Previous Performance Summary

India's external sector showed resilience despite global headwinds. Exports (merchandise and services) reached USD 602.6 billion (6% YoY growth), with strong performance in services and non-petroleum goods. Imports grew by 6.9%, reflecting robust domestic demand. FPI flows were mixed, while gross FDI inflows revived but net FDI declined due to increased repatriation. Foreign exchange reserves remained strong, covering 90% of external debt.

Future Predictions

Global trade is projected to exceed 2022 levels, reaching nearly USD 33 trillion in 2024, driven by services. India's trade deficit is expected to widen. The report highlights the increasing use of NTMs globally, posing challenges for Indian exporters. FPI flows are predicted to remain volatile, influenced by global interest rates and geopolitical factors.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include textiles (aiming for USD 150 billion in exports by 2030), services (especially IT, business services, and potentially travel, transport, and finance), and e-commerce. Companies benefiting include those in pharmaceuticals, electronics, engineering goods, chemicals, and those leveraging MMF in textiles. The PM-MITRA parks initiative aims to attract FDI in textiles.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the paperless e-BRC system, the DGFT Trade Connect e-platform, and the Trade Facilitation Mobile App to improve ease of doing business for exporters. PLI schemes and the NTTM support the textile sector. Initiatives to promote e-commerce exports are also highlighted.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include strong domestic demand, resilient services exports, and positive capital inflows (though volatile). Challenges include the rising global use of NTMs (especially CBAM and EUDR), volatility in FPI flows, and the need to improve the competitiveness of the textile sector by reducing costs and embracing MMF production.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's CAD is relatively contained compared to other G20 economies. The rupee performed better than several other currencies in 2024. India's FDI inflows, while showing gross growth, have been impacted by global trends and increased repatriation. The inclusion of Indian government bonds in global indices is a positive development.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in e-commerce exports, the MMF segment of the textile industry, and various service sectors. The government's initiatives to improve ease of doing business and attract FDI create favorable conditions for investment.

Technology and Innovation Impact

Digital technologies are driving e-commerce growth. The report emphasizes the need for investment in R&D and sustainable production techniques in the textile sector to meet evolving global demands.

Social Implications

The textile sector is a major employment generator, particularly for women and rural populations. Policies aimed at boosting exports and attracting FDI have positive implications for employment and economic inclusion.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's external sector remains resilient but faces challenges from global uncertainties and protectionism. A strategic trade roadmap focusing on reducing trade costs, improving export facilitation, and enhancing sector-specific competitiveness is crucial. Continued deregulation and ease of doing business initiatives are key to attracting FDI and improving investment efficiency. The government's proactive approach, coupled with industry investment in quality and innovation, is vital for achieving sustained economic growth.

SOCIAL SECTOR EXTENDING REACH AND DRIVING EMPOWERMENT

Philosophy of Policy Makers

India's economic strategy prioritizes inclusive growth and citizen empowerment, focusing on education, healthcare, skill development, and social infrastructure. The vision is a 'Viksit Bharat' (Developed India) by 2047, achieved through sound, effective, holistic, and comprehensive policies that translate growth into meaningful development.

Previous Performance Summary

Significant progress has been made in social sector spending, which increased from 23.3% of total expenditure in FY21 to 26.2% in FY25 (BE). Education and health expenditures saw CAGRs of 12% and 18%, respectively, during the same period. The urban-rural consumption gap narrowed significantly, and government welfare schemes demonstrably reduced inequality. However, challenges remain in foundational literacy and numeracy, school retention rates, and equitable access to healthcare and higher education.

Future Predictions

The survey projects continued growth in social sector spending and a narrowing of the urban-rural divide. Increased investment in education and healthcare, coupled with technological advancements, is expected to improve health and educational outcomes. However, challenges related to mental health, non-communicable diseases, and the effective implementation of NEP 2020 need to be addressed.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include education (foundational literacy and numeracy, social and emotional learning, digital literacy, skill development, higher education reform), healthcare (preventive healthcare, technology integration, mental health, NCD prevention), and rural development (infrastructure, housing, livelihoods). Companies involved in EdTech, health tech, rural infrastructure development, and financial inclusion are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the National Education Policy 2020 (NEP 2020), focusing on holistic and multidisciplinary education; the Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (AB PM-JAY), expanding healthcare access; the Pradhan Mantri Awaas Yojana-Gramin (PMAY-G), providing rural housing; and the ongoing efforts to localize the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). The survey also advocates for a 'light but tight' regulatory framework in higher education, emphasizing transparency and outcome-based assessment.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include increased government spending on social sectors, technological advancements in education and healthcare, and initiatives to improve rural livelihoods. Challenges include addressing learning gaps, improving school retention rates, tackling mental health issues, preventing non-communicable diseases, ensuring equitable access to healthcare, and effectively implementing NEP 2020 and other policy reforms. The rising consumption of ultra-processed foods poses a significant threat to public health.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's social sector investments are substantial and aim to achieve universal health coverage and improve educational outcomes, aligning with global development goals. The survey compares India's progress with OECD countries and highlights the need to address the challenges of affordability and equitable access to healthcare, particularly in medical education.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Significant opportunities exist for businesses and investors in EdTech, health tech, rural infrastructure development, and financial inclusion. The government's focus on technology integration creates opportunities for companies providing AI-powered solutions, telemedicine platforms, and digital health infrastructure.

Technology and Innovation Impact

AI, digital transformation, and emerging technologies like drones and telemedicine are playing a crucial role in shaping economic policies and improving service delivery in education and healthcare. The government is actively promoting the use of technology to enhance efficiency, equity, and accessibility.

Social Implications

The policies aim to improve employment opportunities, enhance educational outcomes, improve healthcare access, and strengthen social welfare. However, the survey highlights the need to address the social determinants of health, including lifestyle choices, workplace culture, and mental well-being, to fully realize the demographic dividend.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's social sector policies are ambitious and show progress, but require continuous refinement and effective implementation. A focus on learning outcomes, preventive healthcare, technology integration, and addressing the social determinants of health is crucial. Regulatory reforms should prioritize outcomes over rigid compliance, fostering innovation and transparency. Addressing the rising consumption of ultra-processed foods is vital for long-term health and economic prosperity.

INVESTMENT AND INFRASTRUCTURE KEEPING IT GOING

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The government's core philosophy centers on accelerating infrastructure development as a cornerstone of India's 'Viksit Bharat@2047' vision. This involves significantly increasing investment in physical, digital, and social infrastructure, recognizing that public funding alone is insufficient. A key strategic intent is to foster greater private sector participation through improved risk-sharing mechanisms, streamlined approvals, and innovative financing models like asset monetization.

Previous Performance Summary

The previous five years saw a 38.8% trend rate increase in Union Government capital expenditure on major infrastructure sectors (FY20-FY24). Initiatives like the National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP) and National Monetisation Pipeline (NMP) were launched to expedite project execution and attract private investment. While significant progress was made in several sectors (railways, roads, ports, renewable energy), the pace was affected in Q1 FY25 due to elections and monsoon disruptions.

Future Predictions

The survey projects continued momentum in infrastructure capex post-election. However, it emphasizes the persistent need for substantially increased investment over the next two decades to meet India's development goals. This necessitates innovative financing and a significant increase in private sector participation.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include railways (network expansion, rolling stock, station modernization), roads (highway construction, logistics parks), ports (capacity expansion, port-led industrialization), civil aviation (airport development, regional connectivity), renewable energy (solar, wind), digital connectivity (5G rollout, BharatNet), rural infrastructure (Jal Jeevan Mission, Swachh Bharat Mission), and urban infrastructure (smart cities, housing, transport). Companies involved in these sectors, particularly those with expertise in PPP models and sustainable construction, are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the launch of NIP and NMP, reforms to encourage private investment in brownfield assets, the rechristening of the Universal Service Obligation Fund to Digital Bharat Nidhi, the Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme for power, and the introduction of sustainable practices in highway development and waste management. RERA implementation across states is also a significant regulatory update.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include increased government capex, initiatives to attract private investment, technological advancements (5G, AI), and a focus on sustainable development. Challenges include the need for significantly greater private sector participation, overcoming bureaucratic bottlenecks, managing monsoon disruptions, and ensuring the financial sustainability of infrastructure projects.

Global Impact and Comparisons

The survey highlights India's growing reliance on renewable energy, aligning with global efforts to combat climate change. Comparisons with other economies are made regarding data center capacity and construction costs, showcasing India's competitive advantages in certain areas.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Lucrative opportunities exist in PPP projects across various infrastructure sectors, particularly those incorporating sustainable technologies and innovative financing models. High-growth areas include renewable energy, digital infrastructure, and sustainable urban development. Investment insights emphasize the need for coordinated action among stakeholders to unlock the full potential of private sector participation.

Technology and Innovation Impact

Technology plays a crucial role, with 5G rollout enhancing digital connectivity, AI-powered tools improving urban management, and geospatial technologies aiding infrastructure monitoring. The government is actively promoting digital transformation and the adoption of innovative technologies across various sectors.

Social Implications

The policies aim to improve quality of life through enhanced access to safe drinking water, sanitation, affordable housing, public transport, and healthcare. Employment opportunities are created through infrastructure projects and initiatives like the Haritha Karma Sena in Kerala. The focus on sustainable development has positive environmental implications.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's infrastructure development is a work in progress, with significant achievements but a continued need for substantial investment and increased private sector participation. Success hinges on coordinated action among government agencies, private sector players, and financial institutions to overcome challenges and unlock the full potential of India's infrastructure ambitions. Focus should be on streamlining approvals, improving risk-sharing mechanisms, promoting sustainable practices, and leveraging technology for efficient project management and monitoring.

STATE OF THE ECONOMY GETTING BACK INTO THE FAST LANE

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The policy makers emphasize fiscal discipline, macroeconomic stability, and a focus on sustained growth, leveraging domestic strengths while navigating global uncertainties. There's a clear intent to promote inclusive growth, evident in the focus on rural demand and formalization of the labor market.

Previous Performance Summary

India's FY25 (2024-25) saw 6.4% real GDP growth. Agriculture and services sectors were key drivers, while manufacturing faced headwinds from weak global demand. Private consumption remained stable, supported by rural demand. Fiscal discipline and a services trade surplus contributed to macroeconomic stability. The economy showed resilience in recovering from the pandemic, with aggregate GVA surpassing pre-pandemic trends.

Future Predictions

FY26 (2025-26) growth is projected between 6.3% and 6.8%. Headwinds include geopolitical uncertainties and potential commodity price shocks. Domestically, the success of translating private capital goods order books into sustained investment, improved consumer confidence, and corporate wage growth will be crucial. Food inflation is expected to ease, but global uncertainties remain a risk.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include agriculture (with a focus on record Kharif production and Rabi prospects), services (especially financial, real estate, and professional services), and infrastructure (construction showing strong growth). Companies in capital goods, cement, iron, and steel are expected to benefit from increased investment.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

The report highlights the continued emphasis on fiscal discipline and macroeconomic stability. Regulatory tightening in certain sectors (e.g., unsecured retail loans) is noted. The document emphasizes the need for grassroots-level structural reforms and deregulation to enhance global competitiveness and medium-term growth potential.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include robust services sector performance, rebounding rural demand, and government capital expenditure. Challenges include global manufacturing slowdown, geopolitical uncertainties, potential commodity price shocks, and the need to translate order books into sustained investment.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's growth contrasts with the uneven global picture, showing resilience compared to manufacturing slowdowns in Europe and parts of Asia. India's services sector outperforms the global manufacturing sector's struggles. The report compares India's performance to other advanced and emerging economies, highlighting India's relatively strong growth and stable macroeconomic indicators.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in infrastructure development, capital goods, and the services sector. The report suggests that India's strong external sector and stable macroeconomic environment offer attractive investment prospects. Diversification of export markets and strategic technology partnerships are highlighted as key opportunities.

Technology and Innovation Impact

The report acknowledges the potential impact of AI on India's labor market, emphasizing the need for skill development to leverage AI's benefits and mitigate potential job displacement. The focus is on preparing the workforce for an AI-augmented future.

Social Implications

The report highlights the positive impact of formalization of the labor market on employment and social security. The declining unemployment rate and increased formal sector employment are presented as positive social outcomes. The potential impact of AI on employment is also discussed.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's economy remains fundamentally strong, despite global headwinds. Sustained growth requires continued fiscal discipline, investment in infrastructure, and grassroots-level reforms to enhance competitiveness. Addressing potential challenges in the manufacturing sector and navigating global uncertainties are crucial for maintaining the positive growth trajectory.

LABOUR IN THE AI ERA CRISIS OR CATALYST

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy is proactive and preventative. Policymakers recognize the potential for AI to disrupt the Indian labor market, mirroring historical technological revolutions. Their vision is to use this as a catalyst for equitable economic transformation, not a crisis. The strategic intent is to build robust institutions – enabling, insuring, and stewarding – to manage the transition and maximize benefits while minimizing disruptions.

Previous Performance Summary

The document doesn't explicitly summarize previous performance. However, it implicitly highlights concerns about existing job vulnerabilities in India's service-based economy, particularly in low-value-added sectors susceptible to automation.

Future Predictions

The document predicts significant job displacement due to AI, citing various studies estimating millions of jobs at risk globally and in India. However, it also acknowledges that the timeline and magnitude of this displacement are uncertain due to challenges in AI's scalability, reliability, and resource efficiency. The future depends heavily on the success of institutional reforms.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include services (finance, healthcare, retail, business services), where high demand elasticity suggests potential for job creation despite automation. Key investment areas are education and workforce skilling to equip workers with future-ready skills. Companies with strong AI capabilities and those adapting to AI augmentation are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

The document advocates for building three types of institutions: enabling (skill development and education), insuring (social safety nets), and stewarding (responsible AI governance and ethical frameworks). It emphasizes the need for collaboration between policymakers, the private sector, and academia to achieve this.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include India's demographic dividend and the potential for AI to augment productivity in high-demand sectors. Challenges include the potential for large-scale job displacement, the need for massive investment in education and infrastructure, and the ethical and societal implications of widespread AI adoption. Resource efficiency in AI development is a major bottleneck.

Global Impact and Comparisons

The document compares India's situation to global trends, noting that while emerging economies may be less immediately affected by AI than advanced economies, they still face significant risks. It uses global studies and forecasts to contextualize India's challenges and opportunities.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist for businesses developing and implementing AI solutions responsibly, focusing on augmentation rather than replacement of human labor. High-demand sectors with high elasticity offer the best prospects. Investment in education and skill development is also a significant opportunity.

Technology and Innovation Impact

AI is central to the document's analysis. It highlights the transformative potential of AI but also its limitations, emphasizing the need for a human-centered approach to AI adoption. The document stresses the importance of addressing AI's current limitations in reliability, resource efficiency, and infrastructure before widespread adoption.

Social Implications

The document extensively examines the social implications of AI, focusing on potential job displacement, the need for social safety nets, and the importance of equitable distribution of AI's benefits. It emphasizes the need for education and skill development to mitigate negative impacts on employment and social welfare.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

The key takeaway is that proactive institutional development is crucial for India to navigate the AI revolution successfully. Recommendations include building enabling, insuring, and stewarding institutions, investing heavily in education and skill development, fostering collaboration between stakeholders, and promoting responsible AI development and deployment. The next steps involve implementing these institutional reforms and monitoring the impact of AI on the labor market closely.

MONETARY AND FINANCIAL SECTOR DEVELOPMENTS THE CART AND THE HORSE

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy emphasizes balanced growth and stability in India's monetary and financial sectors. The strategic intent is to foster inclusive growth, leveraging technology and innovation while mitigating risks associated with rapid financial sector expansion and potential financialization.

Previous Performance Summary

India's monetary and financial sectors showed strong performance in FY25's first nine months. Bank credit growth converged towards deposit growth, profitability of scheduled commercial banks (SCBs) improved (lower GNPAs, higher CRAR), and financial inclusion significantly advanced (RBI's Financial Inclusion Index rose). Rural Financial Institutions (RFIs) and Development Financial Institutions (DFIs) played crucial roles. Capital markets performed strongly, exceeding emerging market peers despite global uncertainties. However, rising consumer debt and unsecured lending emerged as potential risks.

Future Predictions

India's credit-to-GDP ratio is below its trend line, suggesting sustainable credit growth. The insurance sector is projected to be the fastest-growing among G20 nations in the next five years. However, potential risks include a US market correction impacting Indian markets, and the need to manage the balance between financial sector growth and potential financialization.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include infrastructure (supported by DFIs like NaBFID and IIFCL), agriculture (with increased collateral-free loan limits), MSMEs (showing faster credit growth than large enterprises), and the financial technology sector (growing rapidly in GIFT City). Companies benefiting include those leveraging technology for financial services and those involved in infrastructure development.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include a neutral monetary policy stance, a CRR cut, increased interest rate ceilings on FCNR(B) deposits, and increased collateral-free agricultural loan limits. The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) has been instrumental in resolving financial distress. The government is also focusing on improving the efficiency of the IBC and strengthening the National Company Law Tribunals (NCLTs). The introduction of the Unified Pension Scheme (UPS) is a significant reform in the pension sector.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include strong macroeconomic fundamentals, robust domestic institutional investment, increased retail participation in capital markets, and technological advancements (UPI, OCEN, ULI). Challenges include managing rising consumer debt, unsecured lending, potential US market corrections, and the risk of financialization.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's stock market consistently outperformed emerging market peers, although its credit-to-GDP ratio remains lower than in advanced economies. India's insurance sector is projected to grow faster than other G20 nations. The Indian market shows increasing resilience to US market movements, but remains sensitive to major global shocks.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in infrastructure financing, fintech, and the insurance sector. The growing retail investor base presents opportunities for investment in the equity market, although caution is advised due to potential market corrections. GIFT City offers opportunities for international financial institutions.

Technology and Innovation Impact

AI, digital transformation, and UPI are driving financial inclusion and efficiency. However, risks associated with AI in banking require robust governance frameworks. The RBI is actively addressing these challenges through initiatives like the regulatory sandbox and the FREE-AI committee.

Social Implications

Financial inclusion initiatives are empowering rural populations and promoting economic equality. The pension sector reforms aim to improve retirement security, particularly for the informal sector. However, the rise in consumer debt needs careful monitoring to prevent over-indebtedness.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's financial sector is poised for continued growth, but requires careful management of emerging risks. Strengthening regulatory frameworks, promoting financial literacy, and leveraging technology are crucial for sustainable and inclusive growth. A balanced approach is needed to avoid the pitfalls of financialization while fostering innovation and competition.

PRICES AND INFLATION UNDERSTANDING THE DYNAMICS

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy centers on achieving price stability and managing inflation effectively to ensure economic recovery and protect consumers. Policymakers aim to balance inflation control with sustainable economic growth, employing a mix of monetary and fiscal interventions.

Previous Performance Summary

Global inflation peaked in 2022 but declined afterward due to policy measures. In India, retail inflation eased in FY25, with core inflation reaching a decade low. However, food inflation remained a challenge, particularly due to volatile prices of onions, tomatoes, and pulses, driven by supply chain disruptions and extreme weather.

Future Predictions

India's retail inflation is projected to align with the target in FY26, aided by declining global commodity prices. The RBI and IMF predict inflation rates around 4-4.8% in FY25 and 4-4.2% in FY26. The World Bank forecasts a decline in commodity prices, further easing inflationary pressures.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include agriculture, particularly pulses, oilseeds, tomatoes, and onions. Companies involved in climate-resilient agriculture, improved storage and transportation, and efficient supply chains for these food items are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include government interventions to control prices of vegetables (buffer stocking, subsidized sales), pulses (subsidized sales, stock limits, import easing), and cereals (stock limits, open market sales). The budget also emphasizes large-scale vegetable production clusters and farmer-producer organizations.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include a projected decline in global commodity prices and increased Kharif food grain production. Challenges include the vulnerability of vegetable production to extreme weather events, persistent deficits in pulses and oilseeds, and post-harvest losses.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's inflation trends are generally aligning with the global downward trend, although food inflation diverges due to domestic factors. The country's reliance on domestic production for key food items limits its ability to fully leverage global price declines.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in developing climate-resilient crops, improving supply chain efficiency for perishable goods, investing in advanced storage and transportation technologies, and supporting farmer-producer organizations. Companies focusing on data-driven insights into agricultural markets can also thrive.

Technology and Innovation Impact

The report highlights the need for robust data systems to monitor prices and inform policy decisions. Technological advancements in climate-resilient agriculture, precision farming, and supply chain management are crucial for long-term price stability.

Social Implications

Price stability directly impacts household budgets and consumer welfare. Policies aimed at controlling food inflation have a significant impact on the affordability of essential food items for the general population.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

Long-term price stability requires a multi-pronged approach: developing climate-resilient crops, improving agricultural practices, strengthening data collection and analysis, and enhancing supply chain efficiency. Government interventions, while effective in the short term, need to be complemented by long-term strategies to address structural issues in agriculture.

CLIMATE AND ENVIRONMENT ADAPTATION MATTERS

Philosophy of Policy Makers

India's policy philosophy centers on achieving developed nation status by 2047 through inclusive and sustainable development. This involves pursuing low-carbon growth despite vulnerability to climate change, prioritizing both mitigation and adaptation strategies. The Lifestyle for Environment (LiFE) initiative underscores a shift towards mindful consumption and production, promoting a circular economy.

Previous Performance Summary

Adaptation expenditure increased from 3.7% to 5.6% of GDP between FY16 and FY22. Significant progress was made in renewable energy capacity, but challenges remain in storage technology and mineral access. Domestic resources have largely financed climate action, while international finance remains inadequate. The outcome of COP29 offered little promise of increased international funding.

Future Predictions

India faces a substantial energy gap to achieve its development goals. The insufficient international funding for climate action may necessitate a reworking of climate targets and impact resource allocation for development challenges. Continued reliance on domestic resources is anticipated.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include agriculture (climate-resilient seeds, water management), urban areas (flood mitigation, green spaces, water recycling), coastal regions (mangrove conservation, sea defenses), and water management (rainwater harvesting, aquifer mapping). Companies involved in renewable energy, green technology, sustainable infrastructure, and water management are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the National Adaptation Plan (NAP) development, the LiFE initiative, the introduction of the Energy Conservation and Sustainable Building Code (ECSBC) 2024, SEBI's enhanced ESG reporting mandates (BRSR), the revamped framework for green debt securities (including transition, blue, and yellow bonds), the Green Credit Programme (GCP), and various initiatives promoting renewable energy (PM-KUSUM, PM Surya Ghar).

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include increased renewable energy deployment, investment in green infrastructure, and the LiFE initiative. Challenges include the lack of viable energy storage technologies, limited access to critical minerals, inadequate international climate finance, the need to balance energy security with energy transition, and managing the end-of-life of renewable energy technologies.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's per capita carbon emissions are significantly lower than the global average. However, developed nations' continued reliance on fossil fuels and insufficient financial commitments to support developing countries' climate action highlight a global equity challenge. India's experience contrasts with developed nations' slower transition to renewables due to cost and grid stability issues.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Significant opportunities exist in renewable energy technologies, green infrastructure development, water management solutions, climate-resilient agriculture, and the circular economy. Investment in research and development related to battery storage, critical minerals, and green hydrogen is crucial.

Technology and Innovation Impact

The report highlights the increasing energy demand from AI and data centers as a new emission source, underscoring the need for technological solutions. Innovation in renewable energy technologies, energy storage, and waste management is crucial for achieving sustainable development goals.

Social Implications

Policies aim for inclusive development, impacting employment through green jobs creation and social welfare through improved sanitation (Swachh Bharat Mission) and access to clean energy. The LiFE initiative aims to change individual behavior for environmental sustainability.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's path to sustainable development requires a balanced approach, prioritizing both mitigation and adaptation. Increased domestic investment in renewable energy technologies, energy storage, and critical minerals is essential. The LiFE initiative needs strong implementation to drive behavioral change. International cooperation and financial support remain crucial, but India must prioritize domestic solutions to achieve its ambitious goals.

PREFACE

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The Economic Survey 2024-25 advocates for a philosophy of deregulation and 'getting out of the way' to unleash India's economic potential. This involves minimizing micromanagement, shifting from a 'guilty until proven innocent' regulatory approach to 'innocent until proven guilty', and fostering trust in the system to encourage innovation and scale. The focus is on empowering businesses and households, recognizing India's unique context and avoiding a blanket adoption of Western models.

Previous Performance Summary

The Indian economy showed steady growth post-Covid, with macroeconomic indicators looking positive. However, challenges remain, including uneven recovery across sectors and concerns about fiscal prudence at the state level. The previous year saw progress in bringing public finances on track.

Future Predictions

The Survey projects continued economic growth for 2025-26 (the exact figure is withheld in the preface), emphasizing a realistic yet ambitious approach. However, the outlook is clouded by a slowing global economy, geopolitical uncertainties, and the end of the era of rapid world trade growth. Domestic growth levers will be crucial.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include infrastructure (especially digital and physical connectivity), manufacturing (with a focus on indigenization and reducing reliance on Chinese supply chains), agriculture (increasing productivity and diversification), services (leveraging technological advancements), and renewable energy (while acknowledging energy security and affordability). Companies involved in these sectors, particularly those focused on domestic supply chain resilience and technological innovation, are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes focus on significant deregulation to lower the cost of doing business and boost employment. This includes rolling back regulations, embracing risk-based approaches, and improving the ease of doing business. Specific measures include stringent front-of-pack labeling rules for ultra-processed foods to address public health concerns and policies to promote electric mobility while mitigating import dependency.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include India's young demographic, strong corporate and financial sector balance sheets, and government investments in infrastructure. Challenges include global economic slowdown, geopolitical risks, rising energy costs, and the need to address supply chain vulnerabilities, particularly concerning China's dominance in certain sectors. The low-trust environment outside of close-knit communities also hinders scale and innovation.

Global Impact and Comparisons

The Survey highlights the global economic slowdown, particularly in Europe and the unexpected lack of growth in China post-Covid. It draws parallels between India's challenges and those faced by Europe, emphasizing the 'China challenge' to competitiveness. However, India's youthful population is presented as a significant advantage.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in sectors focused on domestic supply chain resilience, renewable energy, digital infrastructure, and technological innovation, particularly in AI. Investors should consider companies addressing India's specific needs and those contributing to indigenization efforts.

Technology and Innovation Impact

The Survey emphasizes the importance of AI and digital transformation, but also cautions against uncritical adoption. It highlights the need for responsible AI development and upskilling/reskilling initiatives to mitigate potential job displacement. The government's investment in AI centers of excellence is mentioned as a key policy initiative.

Social Implications

Policies aim to improve social welfare through investments in education, healthcare (including mental health), and skill development. The Survey highlights the need to address the impact of ultra-processed foods and screen time on public health, and to empower women and marginalized communities through economic participation.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

The Survey concludes that India's economic growth requires a significant deregulation push to leverage its demographic dividend. It emphasizes the need for strategic thinking, particularly regarding energy transition and geopolitical vulnerabilities. Stakeholders should focus on adapting to global changes, fostering domestic supply chain resilience, and embracing innovation while prioritizing public health and social welfare.

INDUSTRY ALL ABOUT BUSINESS REFORMS

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy centers on transforming India into a global manufacturing powerhouse. This involves strategic public investment in infrastructure, fostering innovation and R&D, promoting ease of doing business, and supporting MSMEs. The approach acknowledges the need for coordinated efforts across government, private sector, academia, and R&D institutions.

Previous Performance Summary

India has significantly increased its share of global manufacturing, particularly in sectors like steel, cement, chemicals, and consumer goods (automobiles, electronics, pharmaceuticals). However, challenges include a slowdown in export demand due to geopolitical tensions, supply chain disruptions, and a global trade slowdown. The industrial sector experienced growth fluctuations post-pandemic, with a decline in Q2 FY25 attributed to export slowdowns, monsoon impacts, and festival timing variations.

Future Predictions

The report predicts continued growth in the industrial sector, driven by infrastructure projects and government initiatives. However, global uncertainties pose risks to export demand and domestic production costs. Optimism remains, as indicated by business surveys and the PMI.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include core input industries (steel, cement, chemicals, petrochemicals), capital goods, consumer goods (automobiles, electronics, textiles, pharmaceuticals), and MSMEs. The government is actively promoting 'Make in India', 'Digital India', and Industry 4.0 initiatives. Companies benefiting include those involved in infrastructure development, electronics manufacturing, and pharmaceutical production.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the extension of the PLI scheme, the revamp of the CGTMSE, the launch of the Udyam Registration Portal and Udyam Assist Platform for MSME formalization, initiatives to improve IP protection (streamlined processes, fee reductions), and the promotion of Smart Manufacturing and SAMARTH Udyog centers. The Business Reform Action Plan (BRAP) aims to improve the ease of doing business across states.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include government infrastructure spending, domestic market expansion, skilled labor, and policy incentives. Challenges include global uncertainties, import dependency in key sectors, the need for increased R&D spending (particularly private sector participation), and addressing the fragmented nature of sectors like textiles and MSMEs.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's manufacturing growth contrasts with the decline in high-income countries' share. While India's share remains significantly smaller than China's, it presents a substantial opportunity for growth. India's pharmaceutical sector's performance is compared to global leaders like the US, Japan, and China, highlighting areas for improvement in R&D and innovation.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in infrastructure development, electronics manufacturing (particularly components and design), pharmaceuticals (biopharmaceuticals and new drug development), and the MSME sector. The government's focus on ease of doing business and various incentive schemes creates a favorable investment climate.

Technology and Innovation Impact

The report emphasizes the need for increased R&D spending and innovation, particularly in applied research. Initiatives like Smart Manufacturing, Industry 4.0, and the promotion of IP protection are key to driving technological advancements. The government is leveraging AI/ML in IP processes.

Social Implications

The policies aim to create jobs, particularly in the MSME sector, and improve the skill levels of the workforce. The focus on healthcare through pharmaceutical and medical device development has significant social implications. However, addressing regional disparities in industrial development is crucial for equitable growth.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's ambition to become a manufacturing powerhouse requires sustained, coordinated efforts. Key recommendations include increased private sector R&D investment, state-level business reforms to improve the ease of doing business, targeted support for MSMEs, and addressing skill gaps. A focus on deregulation, innovation, and adapting to global uncertainties is crucial for long-term success.

AGRICULTURE AND FOOD MANAGEMENT THE SECTOR OF THE FUTURE

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy centers on ensuring food security and doubling farmers' income through a rights-based approach, promoting sustainable agricultural practices, and leveraging technology and diversification. The vision is a resilient and high-growth agricultural sector contributing significantly to India's overall economic prosperity and global food security.

Previous Performance Summary

India's agricultural sector showed robust growth (averaging 5% annually from FY17 to FY23), despite weather variability. While cereal production is high, crop yields lag global leaders. High-value sectors like horticulture, livestock (12.99% CAGR), and fisheries (13.67% CAGR) drove growth. However, oilseed growth was slower (1.9%), highlighting import reliance. Inter-state performance varied significantly, with Andhra Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu leading in growth.

Future Predictions

Changing dietary preferences towards horticulture, livestock, and fisheries will shape future growth. Effective post-harvest management and marketing infrastructure are crucial. Climate change poses a significant threat, with potential yield losses due to droughts and heat waves. A 5% annual growth rate with a 20% share of overall GVA is projected, contributing 1% to overall GVA growth.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include horticulture, livestock, fisheries, and food processing. Key investment areas are micro-irrigation, climate-resilient seeds, post-harvest infrastructure, digital agriculture, and strengthening Farmer Producer Organizations (FPOs) and cooperatives. Companies involved in these areas, particularly those supporting small and marginal farmers, are likely to benefit.

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key policy changes include the continued implementation of the Doubling Farmers' Income (DFI) report recommendations, the Per Drop More Crop (PDMC) initiative, the National Mission on Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA), the Digital Agriculture Mission, e-NAM, PM-KISAN, MSP increases for key crops, the Kisan Credit Card (KCC) expansion, the Kisan Rin Portal, Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY), the Agriculture Infrastructure Fund (AIF), and various schemes promoting food processing and organic farming.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include government initiatives, diversification into high-value crops, technological advancements, and increased credit access. Challenges include climate change impacts (droughts, heat waves), water scarcity, low crop yields compared to global averages, and the need for improved post-harvest management and marketing infrastructure. Addressing the overproduction of cereals and underproduction of pulses and edible oils is crucial.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India is a major global cereal producer (11.6% of world output), but crop yields lag behind other leading producers. The growth in high-value agricultural exports (e.g., grapes, floriculture) demonstrates India's potential in global markets. The document compares India's micro-irrigation adoption rates with those of the USA and China, highlighting the need for accelerated progress.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Opportunities exist in developing climate-resilient technologies, improving post-harvest infrastructure, providing financial services to farmers, supporting FPOs, and investing in the food processing sector. Export-oriented agriculture, particularly in high-value crops and processed foods, presents significant potential.

Technology and Innovation Impact

Digital technologies (e-NAM, Kisan Rin Portal, PMFBY technological interventions, drones for SHGs) are playing a crucial role in improving efficiency, transparency, and access to information and services. Research on climate-resistant seeds and precision agriculture techniques is vital.

Social Implications

Policies aim to improve farmers' incomes, enhance social security (PM-KISAN, PMKMY), and create employment opportunities, particularly in allied sectors and through FPOs and SHGs. The focus on small and marginal farmers addresses inequality and promotes inclusive growth.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's agricultural sector holds immense potential for growth, but addressing climate change, improving water management, enhancing market efficiency, and supporting small and marginal farmers are crucial. Policy shifts towards market-driven production, price risk management, and sustainable farming practices are needed to unlock this potential and achieve food security for India and contribute to global food security.

SERVICES NEW CHALLENGES FOR THE OLD WAR HORSE

Philosophy of Policy Makers

The core philosophy centers on leveraging India's robust services sector to fuel domestic and global growth, particularly amidst global manufacturing slowdowns. The strategic intent is to build a resilient economy by promoting digitalization, skill development, and streamlined governance, fostering inclusivity and competitiveness.

Previous Performance Summary

India's services sector consistently contributed over 50% to GVA, exceeding 55% in FY25. While services exports grew steadily, reaching 4.3% of the global market share in 2023, the pandemic impacted FY21 growth. Logistics regained pre-pandemic momentum through digitization. The IT sector showed resilience, with revenue exceeding USD 254 billion in FY24. However, high service inflation due to wage growth posed a challenge.

Future Predictions

The services sector is projected to remain a key growth driver, compensating for potential manufacturing slowdowns. Global services activity is expected to improve, though high inflation persists. India's services exports are poised for continued growth, but face potential risks from evolving global policies and concerns about offshore work quality.

Focus Areas and Sectors

Priority sectors include IT/ITeS, financial services, real estate, logistics (railways, roads, aviation, ports), and tourism. Companies involved in digital technologies, AI, fintech, and those providing services to manufacturing are likely to benefit. The government is actively promoting the Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC).

Key Policy Changes and Reforms

Key reforms include the abolition of angel tax, streamlining transfer pricing, reducing GST on data center exports, promoting digital tolling (FASTag), introducing REITs, the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, and the PM Gati Shakti initiative for multimodal connectivity. ONDC aims to create a more inclusive digital commerce ecosystem.

Growth Drivers and Challenges

Growth drivers include strong domestic demand, increasing servicification of manufacturing, digital transformation, and government initiatives. Challenges include high service inflation, global geopolitical uncertainty, potential risks to IT services exports due to regulatory concerns, and the need for skill development to leverage AI.

Global Impact and Comparisons

India's services sector is a significant player in the global market, competing with the US, UK, Germany, and others. Its growth contrasts with manufacturing slowdowns in some regions, highlighting its resilience. However, India needs to address concerns about offshore work quality to maintain its competitive edge.

Opportunities for Businesses and Investors

Significant opportunities exist in IT/ITeS, fintech, digital logistics, real estate, and tourism. The ONDC presents a major opportunity for businesses of all sizes. Investment in skill development and digital infrastructure is also attractive.

Technology and Innovation Impact

AI and digital technologies are transforming various sectors, driving efficiency and creating new opportunities. The government is actively promoting AI adoption and digital infrastructure development. C-DOT's role in developing indigenous telecom technologies is highlighted.

Social Implications

The policies aim to create jobs, particularly in the digital sector. Skill development initiatives are crucial for ensuring workforce readiness. ONDC aims to empower small businesses and MSMEs. However, addressing potential job displacement due to automation is important.

Conclusion and Actionable Insights

India's services sector is a key engine of growth, but requires strategic focus on skill development and regulatory reforms to maintain its momentum and build resilience against global headwinds. The government's initiatives, particularly ONDC, are crucial for inclusive growth. Businesses should focus on digital transformation and skill development to capitalize on emerging opportunities.
